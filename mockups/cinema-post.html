Taking yourself
out of the equation.

Before someone else does it for you, and calls it progress.

Two stories were running simultaneously on my feed at 10 PM on a Monday night.

One had missiles in it.

The other had a CEO, a public post, and four thousand people who no longer had jobs. I came to it late. Didn't matter.

Only one of them kept me up that night.

It wasn't the missiles.

Maybe that says something about distance. Or about human psychology. Or about the specific numbness that settles in after too many headlines in too few days. My heart goes out to everyone living inside that fire. This post is about a different kind of threat.

Scene 02

A confession, before we go further

I have been quietly automating my own job for the last fourteen months.

Not because I was told to. Because I noticed a pattern: every time a tool got better, my company got more efficient, and the conversation about headcount started to sound less hypothetical.

If your job can be removed from the equation, the only question worth asking is who gets to do the removing. — a thought, between two meetings

Once I asked that question, the answer was obvious. I would rather be the architect than the variable.

Scene 03

The tools that survived.

I tried everything. Plugins, extensions, frameworks promising "agentic" anything. Most of it was theater. A few things stayed in the workflow for more than a week. Even fewer became part of the daily rhythm.

Here is what survived the fourteen months: Claude for the real reasoning. Cursor for everything adjacent to code. Gemini for the second opinion I trust to disagree with me. NotebookLM when the document set is too big for any single context window.

What didn't survive: every tool whose pitch deck was longer than its documentation.

Scene 04

One more thing. About Claude, specifically.

The pattern that changed everything was small. A file in the project root called CLAUDE.md, holding context, conventions, and the boring rules of the codebase. Updated every four or five meaningful interactions.

The model is not the asset. The briefing is the asset.
